Prepare for Class 6

Graphical Perception & Design Reviews

How to use this file: These are my completed notes and answers for discussion. I kept them concise, specific, and actionable.

1. Reading Notes

Required: Cleveland, W. S., & McGill, R. (1984). Graphical perception: Theory, experimentation, and application to the development of graphical methods. JASA, 79(387), 531–554.

Optional: Cleveland (1993) Visualizing Data; KH Ch. 2–4.

1.1 Core Ideas (3–5 bullets)

  • Perceptual task hierarchy: Judgments are most accurate with position on a common scale, then position on nonaligned scales, length, angle/slope, area, volume, and finally color hue/saturation. Design should push comparisons toward the top of this ladder.
  • Experimental method: Subjects made pairwise quantitative judgments across encodings. Error increased as encodings moved down the hierarchy; direct labeling and consistent baselines improved accuracy.
  • Implications: Prefer dot plots, bars (zero baseline), and aligned lines over pies, bubbles, and 3D shapes. Use small multiples for multi-category trends instead of dual y-axes.
  • Caveats: Context matters—range, aspect ratio, and scale choice can change difficulty; over-aggregation and clutter add cognitive load even with “good” encodings.

1.2 Examples (good vs. weak)

  • Good: A small-multiple dot plot comparing graduation rates by district and year on a shared x-axis. Viewers compare position quickly and precisely.
  • Weak: A pie chart showing six programs’ budget shares that differ by only 1–3%. Angles are hard to discriminate; replace with a sorted bar chart (0 baseline), add direct labels and percent signs, and show total n for context.

2. McKinsey “Week in Charts” & “Year in Charts” (2024)

(I reviewed multiple pieces from each series and focused on recurring design patterns rather than one-off exceptions.)

2.1 Quick Scan — What they do well

  • Clear headlines that state the takeaway (“X overtakes Y by 2028”).
  • Annotation-first style: callouts, arrows, and direct labels reduce legend hopping.
  • Tidy scales with restrained grids; frequent use of small multiples for regional splits.
  • Sane color use: neutrals for context, accent color for the focal series.

2.2 Side-by-Side Compare (Picked representative patterns)

Create a small comparison table:

Annotation Short text plus direct labels at ends of lines Longer captions; subtitles add context and caveats Annotation density higher in yearly roundups.
Cognitive load Low—single idea per chart Medium—comparisons across sectors/regions Yearly: add section dividers and mini legends to chunk info.

2.3 What could be better (actionable fixes)

  • Replace remaining pies and stacked area with bars + small multiples when precise part-to-whole or category change matters.
  • Favor direct labeling over color legends in dense multi-series lines.
  • Standardize y-axis ranges across small multiples to support scanning.
  • Where uncertainty is material, add CIs/ribbons or data source notes near the chart.
  • Add accessible alt text and ensure colorblind-safe palettes (e.g., avoid red–green pairings).

3. Library Guides — What to Borrow

Links: - Duke University Library Data Viz Guide: https://guides.library.duke.edu/datavis
- UC Berkeley Data Visualization: https://guides.lib.berkeley.edu/data-visualization

3.1 Takeaways from Duke

  • Start with the question and audience; write the one-line message first.
  • Prototyping loop: sketch → build minimal → get feedback → iterate.
  • Ethics & accessibility: show sources, methods, and uncertainty; meet color contrast; label units.
  • Maintain a reproducible workflow (document code, versions, and data cleaning).

3.2 Takeaways from Berkeley

  • Pipeline mindset: problem → data → encoding → iteration → evaluation.
  • Prefer position/length encodings; avoid 3D and dual axes.
  • Build for readability at small sizes (12–14pt text, direct labels), and add alt text and ARIA for web.

3.3 Answer: What are the “Four Pillars of Visualization”?

Phrasing varies across sources. I adopt this framing, which is consistent with the guides above and the Cleveland–McGill evidence:

  1. Purpose — What question and who is the audience? Define the one-sentence takeaway and the action it should enable.
  2. Data — Are the data suitable, trustworthy, and ethically sourced? Document cleaning, units, uncertainty, and limitations.
  3. Design — Choose the strongest encodings, honest scales, and uncluttered layouts; use small multiples and direct labels.
  4. Narrative & Annotation — Titles, subtitles, callouts, and captions that tell the reader exactly how to read the chart and why it matters.

4. My Design Checklist (for upcoming assignments)

  • Start with the question & audience; write the takeaway first.
  • Choose position/length encodings before angle/area/color; avoid 3D.
  • Use honest scales; show zero for bars or justify exceptions; avoid dual axes.
  • Direct labels where possible; minimize legend hopping; annotate the story beats.
  • Accessibility: colorblind-safe palette; sufficient text contrast; alt text for web.
  • Show uncertainty and sample sizes where relevant.
  • Prefer small multiples over cluttered single panels.
  • Keep data-ink high; remove chartjunk; test with a non-expert peer.

6. Reflection (3–5 sentences)

I will convert any pie/stacked-area comparisons in my drafts to sorted bars or dot plots with direct labels. I will standardize axes across small multiples and keep bar charts pinned to zero unless a non-zero baseline is defensible and clearly signposted. I will add uncertainty ribbons or CIs when readers could otherwise over-read a trend. Finally, I will run a quick peer test: can a non-expert state the one-sentence takeaway in 10 seconds?
